Projects

Learning in Motion

Dynamic Interactions for Increased Trust in Human-Robot Interaction Games - 2020

This project seeked to understand how human embodiment can help develop and increase trust in human-robot interactions. We conducted a study where participants were tasked with memorizing greek letters associated with dance motions with the help of a humanoid robot. We found that participants that participants' trust in the robot increased at a higher rate during human embodiment of motions as opposed to utilizing a touch screen device.


This work was presented virtually at the 29th IEEE International Conference on Robot & Human Interactive Communication (RO-MAN 2020).

Shimi

Robotics Research Project - 2020

This robot was designed to interact musically with people through a variety of modalities. Equipped with speakers, voice recognition, and 3 degrees of motion, we used this robot to test the how different robotic voices would impact a user's perception of the robot.

Human Trust after Robot Mistakes

Study of the Effects of Different Forms of Robot Communication - 2019

We investigated the effect that different forms of communication have on trust with and without robot mistakes during a cooperative task. Participants played a memory task game with the help of a humanoid robot that was designed to make mistakes after a certain amount of time passed. The results showed that participants' trust in the robot was better preserved when that robot offered advice only upon request as opposed to when the robot took initiative to give advice.


This work was presented at the 28th IEEE International Conference on Robot & Human Interactive Communication (RO-MAN 2019).

VeggieBot

Northwestern Design Competition 2018

This autonomous robot detected retroreflective cubes and cylinders blocks, captured the block, identified the shape, and placed it in the respective scoring bin. Some features include pushbuttons, lasers, phototransistors, a distance IR sensor, a vive sensor for location mapping, and a 3D printed gripper.


VeggieBot won first place out of eight Northwestern Teams.

FightyBox

RoboBrawl 2018

This robot was the first time Northwestern has built and competed in combat robotics. The experience allowed us to grow and learn core mechanical and manufacturing design principles.


FightyBox won three of its matches in a double elimination bracket.

MRDC

Midwestern Robotics Design Competition 2017

This three by three foot robot captured soccer balls and placed them in a scoring bin. This was my first experience building a robot on such a large scale and taught me a lot about team building and creating components that work together.


This robot won the Most Creative Design Award from schools across the Midwest.
